How the SNAP screening works

This screening helps someone on SNAP figure out whether the ABAWD work rules apply to them. It asks a few plain-language questions and, based only on the answers, shows one of several outcomes. Nothing is sent anywhere — the answers stay on the person's own device.

What are the "ABAWD work rules"?

ABAWD stands for "Able-Bodied Adult Without Dependents." Federal rules can require some SNAP recipients in this group to work, train, or volunteer a set number of hours each month to keep their benefits. But many people are exempt, and others have a good reason for months they couldn't meet the hours. The screening exists because a lot of people are told they must meet the rules when they actually don't have to.

The possible outcomes

✓
Exempt — you do not have to meet the work rules
The person picked at least one reason that makes them exempt (for example, living with a child under 14, a health reason, or earning enough income). The result lists those reasons and gives a short "Tell DTA" form to send.
✓
Already meeting the work rules
The person is working enough hours (30+ per week) to meet the rules even if their pay is below the income exemption. They should keep reporting work to DTA.
🗓️
Good cause — you may have a good reason for missing hours
No exemption, but a temporary hardship (like losing transportation, a family emergency, or an unfair job situation) may excuse months of missed hours.
📋
May need to meet the work rules
No exemption and no good cause came up. The result explains the ways to meet the rules — working/training 20 hours a week, or volunteering a set number of hours.
ℹ️
Age may not apply
If the person is not between 18 and 64, the screening explains that these rules may not apply and suggests contacting DTA or legal aid.

Why four different result screens?

At first glance, it looks like there should only be three answers: exempt, good cause, or must meet the rules. The tool adds a fourth main result — already meeting the rules — because the work question has more than one kind of “good news,” and mixing them up can hurt someone’s case.

The work question asks whether someone is working enough. Two answers mean you do not have to meet the rules at all (an exemption). One answer means you do have to meet the rules, but you are already doing it by working 30+ hours a week. Those are not the same thing at DTA:

  • Exempt (income) — earning $217.50+/week or 14.5 hours at $15+/hour. DTA should treat the person as exempt from ABAWD rules.
  • Already meeting (hours) — working 30+ hours/week at lower pay. The person is not exempt, but they are complying. They still need to report work to DTA so benefits stay active.

Showing “already meeting” as its own screen avoids telling someone they are “exempt” when they actually need to keep reporting hours. A fifth early result — age may not apply — appears if the person is not 18–64 and stops the screening before the questions.

How the tool decides

The logic is simple and runs entirely in the browser:

  • Exemption answer → Exempt. The questions cover things like living with a child under 14, a health reason, caretaking, pregnancy, a safety situation, Tribal membership, certain benefits (TAFDC, disability benefits, unemployment, state-agency services), being in school, or earning enough income ($217.50/week or 14.5 hours at $15+/hour). If someone does not have a regular place to sleep, a follow-up asks about diploma, health care, work or school history, and recent hospitalization — those answers can also show an exemption. Selecting only "Other" on the disability list does not count as an exemption.
  • Working 30+ hours/week → Already meeting the rules. This is different from an exemption — the person is complying with the hours requirement.
  • No exemption, but a hardship → Good cause. A final question asks whether something recently made it hard to work, go to school, or volunteer.
  • Not age 18–64 → Age may not apply. The intro asks age before the screening starts.
  • Neither → May need to meet the rules.

Privacy

There is no backend and no analytics. Answers are kept only on the person's device (in browser storage), are automatically erased after one day, and can be deleted at any time with a button on the results screen. The "Tell DTA" form fields are not saved on the device — the person prints or downloads to keep a copy.

This tool gives general information only. It is not legal advice, and results are based only on the answers given. People should always check with DTA before acting, and can reach a legal aid program for advice.
